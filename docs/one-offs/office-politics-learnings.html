The Prework · Office Politics Field Notes

Work gets you in the room. The prework decides what happens there.

Six learnings on winning decisions before the meeting — pulled from Sara McNamara's playbook and pressure-tested against this week's bottleneck.

The "prework" is the private, one-on-one groundwork you do before a meeting — figuring out who really decides, then lining up their support in advance — so the decision is effectively made before anyone sits down.

The real vote happens before the meeting

You can be right and still lose. By the time everyone sits down, the decision is mostly made — in DMs, 1:1s, and five-minute hallway catch-ups. Stop polishing the deck and start counting the vote early.

Map the room by power — then by failure mode

The org chart says who reports to whom, not who decides. Find the one real Decider, the Shapers whose opinion they quietly borrow, and the Blocker who can only say no. Then add the step Sara skips: ask how each one fails.

Your wrinkle Your Decider (Heather) is also your Blocker. She says yes, then blocks by going quiet — the scorecard was signed off and re-promised, then simply not sent. A Decider who goes silent after "yes" needs a different tactic than one who argues.

Sell to their currency, not your logic

Nobody backs a proposal because it's correct. They back it because it moves something they care about — or protects them from something they fear. Same proposal, a different pitch for each person.

You know the currency Heather's is reputation (why she won't drop the IRH handle) and a hatred of padded, vanity metrics (why FY27 targets were left off). Frame every ask as protecting those — not as "here's the right answer."

Where the playbook goes blind — and you don't

Winning the vote isn't the finish line

Sara's playbook ends at the meeting — "the vote already happened, the meeting is a formality." But in ops the more common pain is the decision that's won and still doesn't move, because it's parked on one person.

Your proof The scorecard: signed off, shown to the team, re-promised the same day — and four hours later, nothing. The prework has no answer for the post-decision bottleneck. That gap is your sharpest angle.

Overwhelm is a map of other people's inboxes

The "I'm behind" feeling usually isn't workload. Inventory two columns — what's actually yours to move vs. what's waiting on someone else — and the dread collapses to a short list.

Confirmed twice On 7/1 and 7/6 the Sunday-scaries "nothing's moving" feeling turned out to be external-dependency backup, not a real pile of work. You called it "a big one." It's your most original idea — and it's nowhere in Sara's piece.

Relationships get the yes; forcing functions get it done

Prework is all relationship work — but relationships don't execute on their own. Even a same-day promise doesn't reliably fire. Pair every "yes" with a forcing function: a nudge pass, a "thoughts by Friday" one-liner, a dashboard flag.

The recurring fix "Naming a pattern once doesn't fix it." You've diagnosed the Heather bottleneck repeatedly — the fix is a scheduled forcing function each time, not another diagnosis.

Your takeaway

Get a resolution on blockers — don't let them sit

Especially for operations work, the real cost isn't a "no" — it's the open-ended wait. Ops can't run on maybes. So stop waiting for a blocker to resolve itself and force it to one of three things: a yes, a no, or a date.

The move: sort every blocker into mine to move vs. genuinely needs someone else. Act on the first bucket today — no permission required. On the second, attach a forcing function: a dated "proceeding unless you say otherwise." A blocker with no resolution mechanism is just a wait with better branding.
