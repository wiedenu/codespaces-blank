# Stay in the game
> “Do whatever it takes to keep yourself in the game here. Your actions should move you forward.”

**Nice little writing prompt I saved in my Tumblr drafts.** Seems quite apt on a night like this one where, in my too-focused brain, nothing seems to be going our way.

> What do we do when we encounter those roadblocks, those obstacles, the insurmountable walls that keep us from continuing on our path?

**First, is it an obstacle?** Or, more specifically what is the obstacle? Take a step back, as others told me this evening, and look at the situation from the perspective of the Other, the person or entity on the “other” side of the equation. 

> From their point of view, they feel just as entitled or correct in their opinion or decision as I might feel in mine.

**To the original question, is it an obstacle:** this is an extremely important question to ask. Mostly because we humans make problems much larger than they usually are in reality. What’s the worst that could happen in the situation because of the obstacle at hand? 

> Again, take a step back and look at the situation from another perspective.

**Once we’ve done this,** we can think about what is our first action that we must or should take. In my case of the obstacle I’m facing tonight, there’s not much I can do. I can:

  * research the problem  

  * gather resources about the problem  

  * deligate possible solution or action to another, more qualified individual  

  * wait for more information  




**And that’s really all I can do.** As frustrating as it is, and it’s too bad it’s only taken me 14 hours today to get to this point, there’s not much more I can do aside from waiting for more information. Is there anything I can do when I encounter these situations in the future? 

> What can I do so that it doesn’t take me 14 hours to get to the realization of what actions I can take.

**To me, it’s about breaking the pattern.** Today, I followed my normal pattern of encountering the obstacle, being rejected, not having any visible solutions, and then shutting down. 

> It’s like I’m a computer that encountered a bad piece of code and just froze. 

**I need to break this pattern.** I need to follow those steps I outlined above.

**1\. Research the Problem.** What is the specific problem? Break the problem down into small, easily digestible parts. You can’t eat a 16oz steak in one bite; you need to cut it up into small pieces, many bites, in order to eat the entire steak. Problems are the same way. 

> Once you break a problem down into smaller pieces, the problem doesn’t seem as large.

**2\. Gather resources about the problem.** In my case today, this consisted of researching more information about the issue, since I’m not generally familiar with the situation I faced. Resources could consist of:

  * similar problems or situations others have faced  

  * supporting information  

  * possible solutions or tools to help with the obstacle  




**3\. Deligate possible solution or action to another, more qualified individual.** What I did today, at the suggestion of my spouse, was to hand off further action to an outside person who has more experience in dealing with the obstacle I faced. As my spouse told me, there’s nothing I can do that this other person couldn’t do better and with more experience guiding their interactions and solutions. 

> It’s important for us to realize that we don’t always have to face our problems alone. 

Just like we turned to a therapist to help with our marriage and with my depression doesn’t mean we can’t handle it, it means that we are turning to an expert in that specific area to help us succeed.

**4\. Wait for more information.** As I reread “wait for more information,” I thought that this was more “get on with your damn life!” I spent over half of my day today stressing out and worrying about a situation that not only did I not have any further control over, but I was worrying about a situation that I passed on to a more capable individual! This is like me asking for a peanut butter sandwich from Emeril and stressing out about it because I can’t make it myself; I’m more worried about not having control, than I am about the obstacle or the problem.

**Hopefully, I can turn these lessons into action tomorrow and the days after tomorrow.** Taking the time to sit and write my thoughts down have greatly calmed me down, and positioned me for a successful day tomorrow. Let’s pray for a good solution to the problem and cross the bridge of “what if” after we know more.

_As Elsa famously says in “Frozen,” Let It Go._
