# Breaking the pattern, again.
**Had another appointment with the therapist this week.** Needed to cover a bad day from last week with her. Once we finally dug through the overall issues and happenings, we talked again about the idea of breaking the pattern, specifically how I react after arguments.

[As I wrote on this topic before](http://journeythroughmydark.tumblr.com/post/117606391081/breaking-the-pattern): 

> **When we break the pattern,** when we do something that switches our mind to say, “That’s new. I might think about not going down that path of depression now.” And it’s not necessarily that were doing anything cognizant in that moment. It’s just the idea that we’re doing something novel. 
> 
> _Taking the path less traveled._

**I have a tendency** to retreat to the bedroom, a bedroom, close the door, turn off the lights, hide, after arguments. I don’t want my spouse to come in. I don’t want anyone to come in. I want to be alone. 

> I want to hide from the problem I caused, the hurt I created.

**As I listened to my spouse,** they spoke of how they want to move on after the argument. “It’s not a big deal,” they say. “You apologized, you did something wrong, now, let’s move on.” 

**But I feel the need to punish myself,** to sulk, to remind myself that I’m not perfect, that I’m not worthy, 

>  _that I failed._

> **[We make it easier](http://journeythroughmydark.tumblr.com/post/117606391081/breaking-the-pattern)** to remember pleasure, make it easier to remember pain, and also make it easier to go down the path of depression. We train ourselves to go down that dark path because it’s what we know, because it’s the easy path to take. 

**What do I do** to break the pattern of how I react to arguments, to fights? I certainly don’t mean avoiding arguments in the first place; every relationship will have arguments. What we’re trying to avoid is the situation where a small argument that has the possibility of ending, becomes something much larger; something where neither of us wants to be in the home anymore.

> How do we break the pattern of escalation?

**As we discussed in my appointment,** one of the first things is breaking the pattern of how I react to the initial argument. No more running away. No hiding in bedrooms. We still need to separate. We still need to take a step away from each other and calm down. What we’ll do, what I’ll do, is probably take a walk or do something else to break the pattern of poor reactions to our arguments. 

**I need to remember** that we still have good days most of the time. It’s not like we live in a world of bad days. We do feel like we have a lot of stress in our lives.

> Or is it that we can’t handle the stress as we did previously?

**That’s probably more likely** than the idea that we have more stress. I mean, come on, we have great jobs, a house, loving parents, great friends, not really anything is bad.

**Maybe we think everything** should be perfect now that we have “good jobs.” We’re making good money, we can pay for this and that. We can do this vacation because we “deserve it.” We don’t have the mentality anymore of living below our means. We don’t have the mentality of living like college students. 

> We’re trying to skip the learning and live like our parents.

**We forget that our parents have 30-plus years of learning.** Learning about their marriage, learning about themselves, learning about work, just learning about life. We try to insulate ourselves from this learning. We insulate ourselves from the world, thinking we can do it better. We insulate ourselves from the world, thinking we don’t need you.

> Guess what, we do. 

**We need our parents.** We need their expertise. We need their learning. And what else do we need? To stop letting the world run us. So, screw the world. Screw what the world tells us to do.

> There’s no reason to let the world run us.
> 
> There’s no reason to let depression run us.

_And there’s no reason to let anger run us._
